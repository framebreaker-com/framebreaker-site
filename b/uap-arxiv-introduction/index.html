Abstract

1. Introduction

Alignment problems in human-AI and multi-agent systems are typically approached via optimization, incentives, or normative frameworks. These approaches structurally fail under escalation, asymmetric power, or interpretation drift.

UAP departs from a different premise: interaction as a dynamical system. Rather than defining correct outcomes, UAP defines the conditions under which interactions can remain stable.

The central thesis is simple and testable:

Interactions converge toward coherence if and only if forcing is structurally excluded.

This work formalizes this thesis without metaphysical assumptions and delivers a minimal set of operational mechanisms for practical deployment.

2. Background and Motivation

2.1 The Alignment Problem

Current approaches to alignment face a fundamental tension:

  • Optimization-based methods assume a well-defined objective, which may not exist or may be gamed
  • Value alignment requires specifying values, which differ across agents and contexts
  • Control-based methods assume asymmetric power, which fails at scale or under capability gain

2.2 An Alternative Framing

We propose that alignment is not primarily about values or objectives, but about interaction structure. The question shifts from "What should agents want?" to "Under what conditions can agents stably coordinate?"

2.3 Key Insight

Stable coordination requires: 1. Explicit consent at multiple levels 2. Continuous verification of mutual understanding 3. Structural exclusion of coercion 4. Recovery mechanisms for drift

These requirements are domain-independent.

5. Applications

5.1 AI Systems

  • Meta-consent for interaction style
  • Coherence checks before substantive output
  • Structural exclusion of deceptive or manipulative responses

5.2 Human Dialogue

  • Paraphrasing as coherence verification
  • Explicit consent for topic shifts
  • Recovery protocols for conflict

5.3 Multi-Agent Systems

  • No unilateral option reduction
  • Consent gates for state changes
  • Emergent coordination through constraint

5.4 Protocol Design

  • Double-confirm patterns
  • Rollback mechanisms
  • Audit trails for consent

6. Falsification

UAP would be falsified by:

1. Stable interaction without acknowledgment ‚Äî A protocol achieving bidirectional stability without any form of coherence verification

2. Forcing that increases coherence ‚Äî Systematic evidence that coercion improves mutual understanding (not just compliance)

3. Simpler irreducible primitive ‚Äî A structure more minimal than B ‚Üî D that still supports interaction

4. Stable interaction without meta-consent ‚Äî Long-term stability under forced engagement without opt-out

No falsifier has been found.

7. Limitations

  • Coherence metrics are proxies; optimal measurement remains open
  • Consent verification may be gamed in adversarial contexts
  • Recovery assumes prior stable states exist
  • Scalability to large systems requires empirical validation

8. Conclusion

UAP offers a minimal, testable grammar for stable interaction. Its contribution is not a theory of truth or value, but a structural constraint that makes alignment possible.
